About Vitis vinifera L. ssp. vinifera cv. Chardonnay clone FPS 04

The Chardonnay FPS 04 clone was chosen for sequencing because it is commonly grown in California and throughout the world. The reference plant is located at Foundation Plant Services, University of California, Davis.

Assembly

Genome assembly was based on a hybrid strategy that used long and short sequencing reads, and that merged three separate assemblies: Canu (Koren et al, 2017), DBG2OLC (Ye et al, 2017) and FALCON-Unzip (Chin et al, 2016). The basis of the assembly was sequence data of 58× coverage of single-molecule real-time (SMRT, Pacific Biosciences) long-reads (Canu), and 162× short-read coverage (DBG2OLC). QuickMerge (Chakraborty et al, 2016). was used to iteratively integrate information obtained from the different assembly methods. The hybrid assembly resulted in a contig N50 of 1.24 megabases (Mb), and application of high-throughput chromatin conformation capture (Hi-C) improved the scaffold assembly N50 to 24.5 Mb. For more detailed information, please see Zou et al (2019).

Annotation

Repetitive sequences were identified with RepeatMasker (Hancock and Zvelebil, 2004) using the repeat library previously developed for V. vinifera cv. Cabernet Sauvignon (Minio et al, 2019). Ab initio prediction of protein-coding genes was carried out with SNAP v.2006-07-28 (Korf, 2004), Augustus v.3.0.3 (Stanke et al, 2006) and GeneMark-ES v.4.32 (Lomsadze et al, 2005). Ab initio predictions were combined with the predictions of Augustus trained with BUSCO genes, as well as the gene models annotated with PASA v.2.1.0 (Haas, 2003). RNA-seq data obtained from public databases were: (1) assembled using both an on-genome strategy, with Stringtie v.1.3.3 (Pertea et al , 2015), and a de novo transcriptome procedure, with Trinity v.2.4.0 (2) clustered with CD-HIT-EST v.4.6 (Li et al, 2006); and (3) filtered with Transdecoder v.3.0.1 (Haas et al, 2013). Experimental evidences (transcripts and proteins) were mapped on the genome using Exonerate v.2.2.0 (Slater and Birney, 2005) and PASA v.2.1.0 (Haas et al, 2003), and together with all the predictions used as input to EVidenceModeler v.1.1.1 (Haas et al, 2008). The annotation was refined and enhanced with alternative transcripts using PASA v.2.1.0 (Haas et al, 2003) and assembled experimental evidence. Models not showing a full-length ORF from start codon to stop codon or showing in-frame stop codons were removed. Transcripts were blast-searched for homologue proteins in the RefSeq plant protein database (ftp://ftp.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/refseq, retrieved 17 January 2017). Functional domains were identified using InterProScan v.5 (Jones et al, 2014). Gene models with no significant blast hit against RefSeq plant protein database (high-scoring segment pair  length < 50 amino acids) and lacking any functional domain were discarded. Gene ontology obtained from InterPro domains and RefSeq homologues with at least 50% of reciprocal coverage and identity were combined using Blast2GO v.4 (Conesa et al, 2005) to assign a functional annotation, gene ontology and enzyme commission descriptions to each predicted transcript. Additional details are provided in Zou et al (2019).
